MEMPHIS MILANO

THE MOVEMENT THAT USED SELF-EXPRESSION AS A WEAPON IN THE REBELLION AGAINST MODERN DESIGN.

The Memphis movement originated in Milano Italy in 1980. The founder, Ettore Sottsass, created the Memphis group as a direct response to the minimalist and modernist movements, which were viewed as boring and stifling to artists who craved personal self-expression in design. The Memphis movement is well-known for the unique and colorful furniture that was designed, and it was one of the first movements with the intention of having form follow function. This concept was highly frowned upon in design and served as a rebellion against overly simplified structures. The following pieces are furniture from the Memphis group that was designed from the years 1980 to 1988.

A CATALOG OF ICONIC PIECES:

Carlton

By: Ettore Stottsass
Year: 1981

This unconventional bookcase is brightly decorated with sleek, shiny laminate. The foundation is built from a sturdy wooden base that has been covered with a grey-speckled, Memphis-style pattern. The simple flat planes of color are juxtaposed by all the extra nooks and crannies. The Carlton is one of the Memphis group's most famous pieces due to its easily recognizable silhouette.

Lodge

By: Martine Bedin
Year: 1982

This console is covered in a pattern that is a staple of the Memphis style. It resembles a cracked black-and-white mosaic and adds zest to an already unusual design. Bright red knobs serve as accents that daringly create contrast. Without a doubt, if this piece was in anyone's living room it would surely act as an intriguing conversation starter.

D'Antibes

By: George J. Sowden
Year: 1981

This cabinet stands almost awkwardly tall with its abnormal leg length. The bold use of primary colors is a signature part of the Memphis style, which is clearly evident in this design. An extremely vivid red and yellow lacquer immediately catches the viewer's attention, and its lanky appearance gives you the feeling that the cabinet could start walking on its own at any second.

Andy

By: Aldo Cibic
Year: 1987

This sideboard sits in a solitary fashion, with its intense red overcoat balancing out the simplicity of the design. A single black knob creates a necessary dynamic that effectively adds character to the otherwise plain drawer. The knob's perfectly round structure and the odd proportions make the design appear cartoonish. The quirky, yet effortless essence of the Memphis movement is captured in this piece.

Max

By: Ettore Stottsass
Year: 1987

This sideboard resembles a maze and walks the fine line between simplicity and abstraction. The patterns on the base create a texture that implies a rough and uneven surface, employing terrazzo tiles that are usually used on floors. Wood and plexiglass are also carefully placed throughout the design. Despite this sideboard having a diverse use of materials, the overall design is impressively cohesive.

Metropole

By: George J. Sowden
Year: 1982

This clock is one of the first Memphis designs that uses toned-down hues to create sophisticated pastels. Although the Memphis movement was known for bright colors, it was also known for retro colors which are neutralized versions of brighter tones that are seen throughout the other designs. Similar to most of the other designs, the structure of this clock is atypical and blocks of differing sizes construct a reverse-tier support system.
